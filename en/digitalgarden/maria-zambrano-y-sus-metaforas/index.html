María Zambrano and the Limits of Reason

Philosopher María Zambrano, like me, was from Málaga. She went into exile in 1939, after Franco's victory and the start of the dictatorship. She didn't return until 1984. Forty-five years away: France, Mexico, Cuba, Italy. She was trained in the European philosophy of her time, which trusted in reason, in method, in the capacity to explain everything with rigor. And then she lived exile. That's the ground poetic reason grows from.

Zambrano proposed poetic reason as a way of thinking beyond strict logic. Not against reason, but beyond its most reduced and demonstrative version. A way of reasoning that doesn't need to turn everything into closed definitions, and that allows itself to coexist with what doesn't quite fit.

"Philosophy doesn't contain the whole person. Neither does poetry contain everything that is human."

In this framework, metaphor isn't a stylistic touch, it's a tool for thinking. When Zambrano writes about exile and calls it a wound, she isn't using a literary device. She is saying something that can only be said this way. The image opens access to an experience that purely conceptual language cannot reach.

To think with poetic reason is to recognize that what we feel, sense, or don't yet know how to explain also belongs to thought. It means letting philosophy approach life without domesticating it, holding the mystery rather than erasing it.

More than an alternative to reason, it is a reminder: not everything is resolved with clarity and method. We also think when we speak in images, when we share what is unfinished, when we let words accompany rather than impose.

At the heart of it: there are ways of understanding each other that don't need explanation. That holding something without resolving it is not a weakness of thought. It is another way of thinking.
