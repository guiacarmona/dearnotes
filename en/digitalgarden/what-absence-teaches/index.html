What absence teaches

For writer Elif Shafak, it was Walter Benjamin who opened up a different way of reading a city. Not just its monuments and streets, but its ruins, its remnants, what has been lost and is somehow still present through its absence. She talks about Istanbul this way: a city layered not only with history but with forgetting. A city that asks you to pay attention to what isn't there.

She connects this to her own biography. "I have this interesting relationship with the French language and with France through its absence in my life," she says. The language was in her hands once. "Like sand between my fingers. It just slipped away." And yet it shaped her. The language she no longer speaks is somehow still part of who she is as a writer.

I find this useful for thinking about notes.

We tend to build systems around presence: what we've captured, what we've linked, what we can retrieve. The Zettelkasten as accumulation. The second brain as a place where nothing is forgotten. But what about the notes we didn't write? The books we read and didn't annotate? The ideas we had in the shower, or on the bus, that dissolved before we could reach for our phone?

Maybe those absences are part of the thinking too.

Not everything that shapes us leaves a trace. Some of the most important reading happens in the negative space. In what we almost understood, what we partially disagreed with, what we can no longer reconstruct but that somehow changed how we see something.

Benjamin, in The Arcades Project, was after exactly this: how to read what a city forgot about itself, what it chose not to monument, what fell through the cracks of official memory.

A note-taking practice might do something similar. Not just record what arrived clearly. But stay attentive to what didn't quite form, to the question you can't yet articulate, the connection you sense but can't name.

Source: Elif Shafak on How I Write with David Perell
