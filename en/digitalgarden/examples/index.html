Digital Gardens: examples and further reading

A digital garden is a personal website where you publish notes, ideas, and essays that are in progress, living documents that grow as your thinking evolves. Maggie Appleton, in her wonderful essay on this (A Brief History & Ethos of the Digital Garden), describes it as organized by topography rather than timelines: instead of a blog's reverse-chronological feed of finished posts, a garden is a network of connected ideas at different stages of development. You wander through it; things link to each other.

The metaphor matters because gardens reward tending over time. Some ideas bloom eventually, some sit quietly for months before they suddenly connect with something else. There is no pressure.

The other thing Maggie points out, which I find important, is what a garden is a response to. We've been conditioned by social media to present ourselves like polished, performative brands. A garden is the opposite: a space you own that shows the mess and the process.

Some gardens I like

  • Mental Nodes from Anne Laure Le Cunff — clean, simple, easy to browse.
  • Unsulee — built on Obsidian Publish. Describes itself as "a glimpse into my rabbit holes," which is pretty much the whole idea.
  • Podge Thomas — built in Notion, another proof that the tool doesn't matter.
  • David Ralph Lewis — 600+ notes.
  • Mt. Solitary and Doubleloop — two very different vibes, both worth wandering through.
  • Maggie Appleton's garden — wonderful, but probably not the right benchmark if you're just starting.
  • Andy Matuschak — more academic and research-focused, but the way notes link to each other is worth seeing.
  • Azlen — calls them "quick ideas."
  • And my favorite, a big inspiration for wanting to do this: Ricardas Kiel's Notizen, in German. Ricardas also describes part of the garden as a glossary, that resonates with me as a translator!
